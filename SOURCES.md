# Sources

Output of phase 2: a vetted source list per surface, to be mined for the pattern catalog in phase 3. Collected July 2026, one research pass per surface; every URL was fetched and verified at collection time. This file collects sources only, the tells themselves get cataloged in phase 3.

## Writing base

- **[Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing)** (WikiProject AI Cleanup, type: catalog, actively maintained 2024-2026): The best-known community catalog of AI prose tells, built by Wikipedia editors from real cleanup cases; covers vocabulary, "not X but Y" constructions, significance inflation, rule-of-three parallelism, formatting and punctuation tells, with examples per pattern.
- **[humanizer](https://github.com/blader/humanizer)** (blader on GitHub, type: skill, actively maintained, v2.8.2 as of mid-2026): The most popular anti-AI-writing agent skill (about 29.8k stars); defines 33 patterns across content, language, style, and communication categories, and adds voice-matching so rewrites do not become sterile. Direct prior art for unslop.
- **[Delving into LLM-assisted writing in biomedical publications through excess vocabulary](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adt3813)** (Kobak, Gonzalez-Marquez, Horvat, Lause, Science Advances, type: paper, July 2025): Large-scale evidence from 15M+ PubMed abstracts identifying excess "style words" that spiked after ChatGPT's release; the most-cited quantitative fingerprint study, also on [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07016).
- **[Do LLMs write like humans? Variation in grammatical and rhetorical styles](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422455122)** (Reinhart et al., PNAS, type: paper, Feb 2025): Goes beyond word lists to grammatical and rhetorical fingerprints (nominalization, missing hedges and engagement markers, uniform register) using Biber's feature framework on GPT-4o and Llama 3; shows instruction tuning amplifies the tells.
- **[LLM writing styles](https://www.refsmmat.com/notebooks/llm-style.html)** (Alex Reinhart, type: catalog, updated June 2026): The PNAS author's curated, up-to-date bibliography of research on LLM stylistic differences, organized by theme with concrete examples; the best single index for keeping the pattern catalog current as models change.
- **[Politics and the English Language](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/)** (George Orwell, The Orwell Foundation, type: style guide, 1946): The canonical counter-reference; its rules against dying metaphors, pretentious diction, and padding map almost one-to-one onto modern slop patterns and give the catalog a positive target, not just a ban list.

## Docs and README

- **[What's wrong with AI-generated docs](https://passo.uno/whats-wrong-ai-generated-docs/)** (Fabrizio Ferri-Benedetti, passo.uno, type: article, April 2025): Widely shared critique by a veteran technical writer (Splunk, Grafana) that names the core tells of LLM documentation, including "READMEtitis" (docs that read like cliched GitHub READMEs), subtle hallucinations, hollow overviews with no product truth, and missing information architecture.
- **[kill-ai-slop](https://github.com/yetone/kill-ai-slop)** (yetone, GitHub, type: catalog): A field guide cataloging 33 visual and copy tells of AI-generated projects (emoji everywhere, gradient-and-badge aesthetics, machine-default marketing copy) by the author of avante.nvim, paired with an agent skill that scans repos and strips the patterns out.
- **[LLVM AI Tool Use Policy](https://llvm.org/docs/AIToolPolicy.html)** (LLVM project, type: policy, adopted 2025): The AI policy of one of the largest open source projects; explicitly regulates AI-generated documentation, comments, and PR descriptions, and frames unreviewed AI prose as "extractive contributions" that cost maintainers more than they give.
- **["An Endless Stream of AI Slop": How Developers Discuss the Burden of AI-Assisted Software Development](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.27249)** (Baltes, Cheong, Treude, arXiv, type: paper, March 2026): Qualitative study of 1,154 Reddit and Hacker News posts on AI slop in software work, with documentation and knowledge-resource degradation as an explicit theme; grounds the pattern catalog in evidence rather than vibes.
- **[Diátaxis](https://diataxis.fr/)** (Daniele Procida, type: style guide): The canonical framework for structuring docs around four user needs (tutorials, how-to guides, reference, explanation); the counter-reference that makes hollow AI overviews and purpose-free "Features" sections legible as failures of documentation intent.

## UI copy

- **[Slop](https://impeccable.style/slop/)** (Paul Bakaus, Impeccable, type: catalog): A catalog of 46 deterministic detection rules for AI-generated interfaces by a well-known developer relations engineer and design tooling author, shipped as a site, CLI, and browser extension. Its dedicated Copy section names generic SaaS buzzwords ("streamline", "empower", "supercharge", "world-class"), em-dash cadence, aphoristic manufactured-contrast copy, and theater framing as instant tells. Also listed under Frontend design for its visual rules.
- **[How to fix the "AI-generated" look in your frontend](https://dev.to/alanwest/how-to-fix-the-ai-generated-look-in-your-frontend-1ahh)** (Alan West, DEV Community, type: article, May 2026): Practitioner critique of the default AI landing page, calling "Built for modern teams" the textual equivalent of the purple gradient. Includes a concrete copy review checklist: ban "Empower", "Unlock", "Transform", kill two-abstract-noun feature titles like "Seamless Integration", require at least one specific claim with a number.
- **[How to Spot AI Writing Tells: 17 Examples + AI Words Blacklist](https://www.oliviacal.com/post/ai-writing-tells)** (Olivia Cal, B2B SaaS copywriter, type: article, February 2026, updated May 2026): Working SaaS copywriter's catalog of AI tells in marketing and website copy: overused verbs ("leverage", "delve", "empower"), cliched adjectives ("seamless", "robust", "cutting-edge"), predictable sentence rhythms, and a blacklist table with before/after replacements.
- **[UX Writing: Study Guide](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-writing-study-guide/)** (Anna Kaley, Nielsen Norman Group, type: style guide, May 2024): Canonical counter-reference from the most cited UX research firm, curating NN/g's evidence-based guidance on short copy: button and menu labels, headings, links, error messages, plain language, and tone. Defines what good microcopy looks like so deviations read as tells.
- **[Mailchimp Content Style Guide](https://styleguide.mailchimp.com/)** (Mailchimp, type: style guide): The most widely imitated public content style guide in the industry (Creative Commons licensed), covering voice, tone, interface writing, and a word list. A benchmark for specific, human, plainspoken product copy against which hollow benefit-speak stands out.

## Code, including tests

- **[The Maintainability Gap: AI Code Quality in 2026](https://www.gitclear.com/the_ai_code_quality_maintainability_gap)** (GitClear, type: report, January 2026): The most-cited industry dataset on AI-assisted code quality, analyzing 623 million code changes from 2023 to 2026. Documents an 81% rise in duplicated blocks, a collapse in refactoring and code reuse, and growing short-term churn, extending GitClear's widely referenced 2024 and 2025 reports.
- **[AI-Generated Smells: An Analysis of Code and Architecture in LLM- and Agent-Driven Development](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.02741)** (Zhu, Tsantalis, Rigby, Concordia University, type: paper, May 2026): Systematic audit of technical debt in AI-generated software by established refactoring researchers. Identifies a distinct "machine signature" of defects, a reasoning-complexity trade-off where stronger models produce more bloated and coupled code, and shows that better prompting does not fix the decay.
- **[Death by a thousand slops](https://daniel.haxx.se/blog/2025/07/14/death-by-a-thousand-slops/)** (Daniel Stenberg, curl, type: policy, July 2025): First-hand maintainer account of AI slop submissions to curl, with 21 annotated real examples of hallucinated vulnerabilities, fabricated file paths, and non-reproducible claims. The canonical primary source behind curl ending its bug bounty in January 2026.
- **[peakoss/anti-slop](https://github.com/peakoss/anti-slop)** (Peak OSS / Coolify maintainers, type: skill, 2026): GitHub Action that detects and auto-closes AI slop PRs using 34 check rules derived from 130+ manually reviewed slop PRs against large open source projects, tuned on Coolify's 120+ slop PRs per month. Directly mineable prior art for concrete PR-level slop signals.
- **[AI-Generated Tests are Lying to You](https://davidadamojr.com/ai-generated-tests-are-lying-to-you/)** (David Adamo Jr., software testing researcher and engineer, type: article, October 2025): Focused treatment of test slop: tautological tests that mirror the implementation instead of the intent, weak assertions, and the false confidence of instant high coverage. The clearest single source on why AI-generated tests pass while proving nothing.
- **[AI writes code faster. Your job is still to prove it works.](https://addyosmani.com/blog/code-review-ai/)** (Addy Osmani, Google Chrome engineering lead, type: article, January 2026): Recognized engineer's guide to reviewing AI-generated code, cataloging red flags such as duplication of existing functionality, removed or missing tests, unnecessary complexity, and authors shipping code they do not understand, backed by industry security and productivity data.

## Frontend design

- **[Slop](https://impeccable.style/slop/)** (Paul Bakaus, Impeccable, type: catalog): The most complete practitioner taxonomy found, cataloging 46 concrete markers of AI-generated UI across typography, color, layout, copy, and motion (thick one-sided card borders, Inter overuse, purple/violet gradients, cards inside cards), with deterministic detection tooling. Bakaus is a widely known web developer (creator of jQuery UI, ex-Google), and the ruleset is directly mineable for a pattern catalog. Also listed under UI copy for its Copy section.
- **[Scoring Show HN submissions for AI design patterns](https://www.adriankrebs.ch/blog/design-slop/)** (Adrian Krebs, type: article, April 20, 2026): Quantitative audit of 1,590 Show HN landing pages against 16 deterministic DOM/CSS tells ("VibeCode Purple", Inter everywhere, glassmorphism, badge-above-hero, icon-topped feature card grids), finding 22 percent heavy and 32 percent mild slop. Widely shared and press-covered, it is the rare data-backed evidence that the sameness is real and measurable.
- **[Improving frontend design through Skills](https://claude.com/blog/improving-frontend-design-through-skills)** (Anthropic Applied AI team, type: article, November 12, 2025): Anthropic's own account of why Claude defaults to purple gradients, Inter, and minimal animation (safe choices dominate web training data) and how a loadable skill counters it along typography, theme, motion, and background vectors. Authoritative on the mechanism and the closest prior art to this project.
- **[frontend-design SKILL.md](https://github.com/anthropics/skills/blob/main/skills/frontend-design/SKILL.md)** (Anthropic, anthropics/skills repo, type: skill): The official skill itself, which names specific generic-default aesthetics to avoid (warm cream plus serif plus terracotta, dark plus neon accent, broadsheet style) and prescribes grounding design in subject matter with a plan-then-critique pass. The primary reference for how an anti-slop design skill is actually written.
- **[Why Your AI Keeps Building the Same Purple Gradient Website](https://prg.sh/ramblings/Why-Your-AI-Keeps-Building-the-Same-Purple-Gradient-Website)** (Pragnesh Gajjar, type: article, October 26, 2025, updated June 2026): Traces the full causal chain from Tailwind UI's indigo defaults through training-data concentration to the median landing page (Inter, purple, three feature boxes, centered hero), then covers prompting remedies including Anthropic's aesthetics prompt. One of the more substantive single explainers of why the look exists.
- **[The Age of Average](https://www.alexmurrell.co.uk/articles/the-age-of-average)** (Alex Murrell, type: article, March 2023): The widely cited pre-AI essay documenting aesthetic convergence across interiors, architecture, cars, faces, and brands, and arguing that sameness creates opportunity for the distinctive. The foundational counter-reference the AI-slop discourse builds on, useful for separating what AI caused from what optimization culture already had.

## Naming

- **[code_transformed: The Influence of Large Language Models on Code](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.12014)** (Xu, Huang, Geng, Wan, Shi, Chen, EACL 2026 Findings, type: paper, June 2025, revised February 2026): Large-scale empirical study of 20,000+ GitHub repos giving the first hard evidence that LLMs measurably shift real-world naming conventions, including the rise of snake_case Python function names (40.7% to 49.8%, 2023-2025) and longer LLM-style function names. Best quantitative anchor for code-naming tells.
- **[The Sci-Fi naming problem: Are LLMs less creative than we think?](https://glaforge.dev/posts/2025/07/22/the-sci-fi-naming-problem-are-llms-less-creative-than-we-think/)** (Guillaume Laforge, creator of Apache Groovy and Google Cloud developer advocate, type: article, July 22, 2025): Systematic investigation showing LLMs across Gemini, Claude, and DeepSeek converge on the same names ("Dr. Thorne" appeared 204 times in 26 generations), traced back to training data statistics. Directly explains the mechanism behind AI name sameness, which transfers to project and product naming.
- **[The 2025 Name of the Year is Elara: The Favorite Name of AI](https://namerology.com/2025/12/15/2025-name-of-the-year-is-elara-the-favorite-name-of-ai/)** (Laura Wattenberg, Namerology, author of The Baby Name Wizard, type: article, December 15, 2025): The leading name-trends analyst documents how AI models converge on the same "liquid," baggage-free names (Elara, Nova, Luna, Aria) and explains why: models average human preference data and land on a statistical ideal. The strongest authority on why AI-chosen names all sound alike.
- **[Naming Conventions Making AI Code Readable](https://blog.vibecoder.me/naming-conventions-ai-code-readable)** (Pranay Joshi, Vibe Coder Blog, type: guide, April 30, 2026): Documents what AI naming defaults look like in practice (semantically empty names like `data`, `item`, `result`, inconsistent verb prefixes such as `getUser` vs `fetchUser` vs `loadUser`, missing boolean prefixes) and how teams enforce fixes via CLAUDE.md rules and lint. One of the few sources cataloging AI defaults specifically rather than generic naming advice.
- **[Naming smells](https://hilton.org.uk/blog/naming-smells)** (Peter Hilton, type: catalog, October 31, 2016): Counter-reference from the best-known "naming things" speaker (SE Radio 278, "How to Name Things: The Hardest Problem in Programming"). Catalogs the exact smells AI reproduces: numeric suffixes (`employee2`), vague nouns (`-Manager`), abstract terms (`data`, `object`), and vague `get` verbs, giving vocabulary for the pattern catalog.
- **[A Philosophy of Software Design](https://web.stanford.edu/~ouster/cgi-bin/book.php)** (John Ousterhout, Stanford, type: book, 2nd edition July 2021): Canonical counter-reference; the "Choosing Names" chapter argues names must be precise and consistent, that generic imprecise names are the most common failure, and that difficulty naming signals a design problem. The positive standard against which AI naming slop (generic, verbose, or grandiose names) gets judged.
