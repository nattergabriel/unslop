# UI copy rules

Overlay for interface copy: labels, empty states, errors, and in-product marketing surfaces (landing pages, onboarding, CTAs). The writing base rules apply; this file holds only the deltas specific to short-form product copy.

## Ban list

**Words**
- `empower`, `unlock`, `transform`, `streamline`, `supercharge`, `leverage`, `seamless`, `world-class`, `enterprise-grade`, `next-generation`, `cutting-edge`, `robust`, `revolutionary`, `game-changer`: inflated marketing register, say what the product actually does instead
- `ninja`, `rockstar`, `wizard`, `unicorn`, `disruption`, `crushing it`: startup-culture clichés, cut them
- `omnichannel`, `funnel`, `incentivize`, `thought leader`, `learnings`, `synergy`, `paradigm shift`: insider B2B jargon that obscures the feature underneath, name the specific thing instead

**Phrases**
- `built for modern teams`: placeholder positioning that fits any SaaS product, name the actual audience and their actual problem

## Rules

### Back claims with something real and specific
Prevent: When you state a benefit, a customer count, or a testimonial, make it something a reader could actually check: a real number, a named source, a specific outcome, rather than a hedge or an emotionally inflated claim with nothing to compare against.
Detect: Find benefit claims with an implied comparison but nothing to compare against (faster, better, more powerful than what), and customer counts or testimonial quotes with no traceable source or concrete outcome attached, then replace each with the real figure or source, or cut the claim if none exists.

### Lead headlines and landing copy with the concrete fact
Prevent: Open a headline, feature title, or hero line with the one specific, concrete fact that's actually true (who it's for, what it does, what changed), rather than a pair of abstract nouns, a placeholder audience tagline, a punchy contrast beat, or a dismissive one-word label standing in for a real description, and let at least one line on the page sound like it was written with this specific product in mind.
Detect: Find feature titles and headlines built from vague abstract nouns that bury the one fact a reader is scanning for, generic "built for" positioning that could sit on any product's page, a contrast-beat tagline repeated at every section break, or a dismissive label swapped in for a plain account of what changed, and rewrite each as the specific claim underneath; if the whole page reads uniformly polished with no sentence specific to this product, add one.

### Name the specific action on buttons and links
Prevent: Label a button or link with the actual action or destination it triggers, and vary the verb by what each control does, rather than defaulting to a generic label or reusing one CTA verb everywhere, unless the heading or body text right next to it already states the consequence.
Detect: Find generic command or link labels with no surrounding text that names the action, and the same CTA verb reused across unrelated buttons on a product, and replace each with wording specific to what that control actually does.

### Errors and empty states inform, they don't perform
Prevent: When something fails or a state is empty, say plainly what happened and what to do next in language the user can read, without blaming them, hiding behind a raw code, spending the message apologizing, forcing a joke, or manufacturing enthusiasm through punctuation in the onboarding copy around it.
Detect: Find error or empty-state copy that only reports failure, blames the user, shows a raw technical code, apologizes at length with no information attached, or leans on a strained pun, plus onboarding copy that condescends or piles on exclamation marks, and replace each with a plain statement of what happened and the next step.
